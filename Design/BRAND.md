# friluftsliv.nu — Brand Strategy

> A design & content foundation for Sweden's home of outdoor life.

---

## 1. Brand Personality

| Trait | Description |
|---|---|
| **Grounded** | Solid and trustworthy as the trails it maps. No hype, no exaggeration — honest, reliable information for every adventure. |
| **Inviting** | Nature belongs to everyone. The brand opens its arms wide — to the seasoned hiker, the weekend cyclist, and the first-time paddler alike. |
| **Clear** | Like a well-marked trail, the experience is never confusing. Information is structured, purposeful, and easy to act on. |
| **Calm** | The quiet confidence of a forest on a still morning. It doesn't shout; it guides. It reassures rather than overwhelms. |
| **Purposeful** | Every feature, every page, every word serves one goal: getting the user outside. Form follows function. |

---

## 2. Voice & Tone

**Speak like:** A knowledgeable friend who has hiked every trail and slept in every cabin — and genuinely wants you to as well. Direct, warm, never corporate.

**Not like:** Over-enthusiastic marketing copy that treats the user as a consumer rather than an explorer.

### Principles
- Use second person — **du/dig**
- Prefer active verbs
- Keep sentences short
- Swedish before English in all UI copy
- A 12-year-old and a 70-year-old should both feel welcome

### Example Phrases
> *"Hitta din nästa rutt — oavsett om det är 3 km eller 30."*

> *"Planera övernattningen. Packa ryggsäcken. Ge dig av."*

> *"Naturen väntar. Vi hjälper dig hitta vägen dit."*

## 3. Visual Direction

### Color Palette

| Name | Hex | Usage |
|---|---|---|
| **Pine** | `#2C4A3E` | Primary — headers, nav, key UI elements |
| **Moss** | `#4A7C59` | Secondary — interactive states, icons |
| **Birch** | `#B5C9A1` | Subtle accents, dividers, secondary text |
| **Mist** | `#EFF4EC` | Background — cards, sections |
| **Sky** | `#A8C4D4` | Contextual — water/paddling routes |
| **Earth** | `#6B5B45` | Contextual — terrain, elevation |
| **Ember** | `#D97B4F` | Accent — CTAs and highlights only, use sparingly |
| **Snow** | `#F8FAF7` | Page background |
| **Ink** | `#1C2B25` | Body text |

> **Rule:** Always maintain high contrast. Accessibility is non-negotiable. Never rely on color alone to communicate meaning.

### Typography

| Role | Style | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Display / Headings** | Serif — warm, editorial | Playfair Display, Lora, Fraunces |
| **Body / UI** | Humanist Sans — clean, readable | DM Sans, Instrument Sans, Nunito |

- Maximum **2 typefaces** in the entire product
- Maximum **4 type sizes** per page
- Line-height: **1.6–1.7** for body text
- Large, confident headings + lightweight body text

### Imagery Style

**Use:**
- Real people in real Swedish landscapes
- Natural light — golden hour, mist, blue hour
- Wide, breathing compositions with negative space
- All four seasons represented (snow, autumn colour, summer green, spring)
- Mixed ages and group sizes — families, friends, solo walkers
- Lightly desaturated — let nature's own palette lead

**Avoid:**
- Stock-photo perfection and posed smiles
- Heavy Instagram filters or artificial color grading
- Only elite athletes or extreme conditions
- City environments or built infrastructure
- Lone hero-on-summit clichés that alienate beginners

## 5. Design Do's & Don'ts

### ✅ Do

- Lead with the **map or search** as the hero — the product IS the utility
- Use **generous whitespace** — let content breathe like a forest clearing
- Make filter/search **fast and tactile on mobile** — most planning happens on phones
- Design **thumb-first** — bottom navigation, large tap targets
- Keep navigation **shallow** — 2 taps max to reach a route or cabin
- Show **elevation, distance, and difficulty** at a glance on every route card
- Use **simple, universal icons** for activities (hike, bike, paddle, ski)
- Use **rounded but not cartoonish** UI components (border-radius: 6–10px)
- Apply **subtle terrain textures** on landing sections for depth
- **Trust the photography** — let it fill the space without heavy UI overlay

### ❌ Don't

- Don't use **dark mode as default** — this is a daylight, outdoors product
- Don't use **energy-drink gradients** or aggressive sports-app aesthetics
- Don't **crowd the map UI** with excessive overlays and toolbars
- Don't use **more than 2 typefaces** or 4 type sizes per page
- Don't let the design **alienate beginners** — no "expert only" visual language
- Don't animate **for the sake of it** — motion must serve navigation or feedback
- Don't use **heavy drop shadows** (keep opacity ≤ 10%) — surfaces stay flat-ish
- Don't rely on **color alone** to communicate information (accessibility)
- Don't **over-gamify** with badges and streaks — this is not a fitness tracker
- Don't let **ads or promoted content** visually blend with organic results

## 6. Component Principles

### Map
The map is the product's heart. It must be fast, uncluttered, and work offline or on poor mobile connections. Overlays should be minimal and dismissible. Route lines should use activity-coded colors (from the palette) rather than a single default.

### Route Cards
Show: thumbnail · activity icon · distance · elevation gain · difficulty pill · region. Nothing more at the list level. Details expand on tap/click.

### Search & Filters
Filters are first-class citizens, not an afterthought. Activity type, difficulty, region, overnight option, and season should all be accessible in one step.

### Cabin & Overnight Listings
Treat these like the route cards — visual-first, scannable, with booking/info CTA clearly separated from content.
